Improv(e) your study start!

Fostering students' life skills development with improvisational theater

Welcome to the website of the teaching innovation project "Improv(e) your study start! Fostering students' life skills development with improvisational theater"

This project was funded by a Comenius Senior Fellow Grant of the Netherlands Initiative for Education Research (NRO) and took place at the Faculty of Psychology and Neuroscience at Maastricht University during the academic years 2022/23 and 2023/24.

What was the project "Improv(e) your study start!" about?

The project aimed to integrate improvisational theater exercises into the mentoring program of our first year students. The goal was to improve their experiences in the first months at university and to foster their resilience and ability to connect. In our complex, fast-paced and globalized world, such life skills have become ever more important. We believe that, as a university, we should not only teach students how to study right, but to also develop the life skills that are necessary to successfully master difficulties within their study program and the challenges in society at large.

The implementation of improvisational theater into the mentoring program was realized in two ways:

  1. Students play improv games in class, that is, in regular group meetings with a designated, trained mentor.
  2. Students are encouraged to engage with a newly developed app which contains fun and inspiring improv games (so called "improv dailies"), reflections, and five short surveys which allow them to track their personal development.

The combination of group meetings and the app was meant to help students to become more flexible, creative, empathic, and resilient, eventually improving their well-being, social integration, and academic achievement.

What is improvisational (improv) theater?

Improvisational theater, often called improv, is a form of live performance where people create scenes, dialogue, and characters spontaneously without a script. It relies on quick thinking, active listening, creativity, and collaboration among performers. If enacted in front of others it is often used to entertain and engage the audience.

For this project, we wanted to keep things easy-peasy and make improv accessible also for people who are not familiar with it or not particularly fond of playing theater. The small and fun exercises and games that we used in the group meetings and in the app are therefore based on the principles of improv, but typically do not involve playing a full scene (in front of an audience). The "improv dailies" in the app were mainly designed to be played either alone or with a partner whenever and wherever one likes. A few games can also be played in a group, however.

Why/How did and do we use improv at the Faculty of Psychology and Neuroscience?

Improvisational theater is increasingly being used in educational contexts due to its numerous benefits for students of all ages. At the Faculty of Psychology and Neuroscience (FPN), improv is considered as a form of experiential learning in order to enhance essential life skills (e.g., communication skills, collaboration, resilience and adaptability).

It encourages active participation and can be used to learn a wide range of subjects by incorporating improvisation into lessons and activities. In the course of the Comenius project (i.e., in the academic years 2022/23 and 2023/24), first year psychology students were introduced to different improv exercises as part of their mentoring program in order to increase resilience and foster connection among peers. The various exercises were conducted on site in the mentor group meetings running from September until December and offered in form of "improv dailies" in a newly developed app.

UPDATE

After the project ended, the faculty was eager to keep the improv group meetings as an integral part of their mentoring activities. Importantly, in 2024/25 the whole Bachelor program in Psychology was subject of a major revision. What is the science behind improvisational theater?

In the past two decades, across a wide range of disciplines (e.g., management, education, medicine, and psychology) educational approaches have changed from traditional knowledge-transfer to a more interactive, experiential approach (Boggs et al., 2007; Kolb & Kolb, 2006), thereby sheding new light onto improvisational theatre and drama education as an experiential teaching and learning method. Experiential learning has been widely evidenced to promote students' metacognitive abilities, self-directed learning, and their capacity to apply newly learned skills and knowledge to real-life problems (Kolb & Kolb, 2006).

Both universities, such as MIT Sloan or Stanford University, as well as businesses like google have recognized that flexibility, creativity and resilience are important virtues that are possible to train with methods of improvisational theatre (e.g., Giardella, 2015; Gibb, 2004; Rimbey, 2017; Vera & Crossan, 2004). In addition, initial research shows that improv theater can reduce students' stress levels and uncertainty tolerance and improve their affective well-being (Felsman et al., 2020; Seppänen et al., 2020). Furthermore, beneficial effects on students' creativity, communication, and deep learning have been evidenced (Berk & Trieber, 2009; Felsman et al., 2020; Lavik, 2020; Toivanen et al., 2011; Watson, 2011). Improv theatre has aslo been used in organizations, showing that it may foster creativity and innovation in teams (Gibb, 2004; Kirsten & Du Preez, 2010; Vera & Crossan, 2005).

Improv dailies

The improv dailies are short, easy and fun little games that are based on the principles of improvisational theater and are designed to stimulate students' flexibility, creativity, empathy, perspective taking, and learning. Students can choose between individual and partner+ improv games that belong to six different categories (be creative, get energy, let go, feel connected, have fun, be positive), depending on what they are up to in that very moment!

The improv dailies were developed by the project team and a group of first-year psychology students in 2023: Maia Golăeş, Lars Iglauer, Fiona Kovac, Ella Rumohr, Ema Vilimaitė, and Nika Vujnović.

The videos were recorded with the help of Thom Frijns from FPN's Marketing and Communication department. The actors in the video are members of theater group Alles Is Drama, a student theater association in Maastricht: Katarzyna Godziejewski, Mylia Hennig, Frida Roper, Ella Rumohr, and Conni Maria Spanier. Although Alles is Drama is an amateur theater association, they only work with professional directors. Besides productions, there are always courses on offer, a great way to improve your acting skills!

Reflections

The five reflections were meant to offer students a moment to step back and think about their recent experiences and themselves as a person. They allow them to view things from a different perspective and challenge them to do things differently in the future. The reflections focus on the two life skills resilience and ability to connect and on three important life outcomes (well-being, social integration, and academic achievement).

The reflection exercises were inspired by or stem from a number of online resources from websites, such as Greater Good in Action, Positive Psychology, and Psychology Today.

Surveys

The five short surveys were about students' personal attitudes and experiences at university. By regularly filling them in, they were able to track their personal growth in the two life skills and three life outcomes mentioned before. Students were able to check out the home screen of the app to view their stats! Students' responses were valuable to us, the project team, for improving our mentoring program.
